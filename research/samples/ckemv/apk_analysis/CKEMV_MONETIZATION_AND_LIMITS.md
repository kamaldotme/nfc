# Harvested EMV Cryptograms — Monetization, Limits, and Practical Constraints

Companion to `CKEMV_HARVESTER_ANALYSIS.md`. This document answers the natural follow-up: once `ckemv-harvester` has uploaded a card's PAN + 20 ARQCs to the operator, **what can the criminal actually do with them, and where do they hit a wall?**

All material here is drawn from public threat-intelligence and academic literature: ESET's NGate writeup (2024), ThreatFabric's "Ghost Tap" reporting (2024), Cleafy's SuperCard X writeup (2024–25), the Cambridge pre-play paper (Bond/Choudary/Murdoch/Skorobogatov/Anderson, IEEE S&P 2014), Galloway & Yunusov's Positive Technologies talks (2019/2020), EMVCo's published specifications, Visa/Mastercard scheme bulletins, ENISA payments-fraud annuals, RBI/ECB/FCA regulator disclosures.

---

## 1. Is this "like a magstripe 101 dump that you can use whenever you want"?

**Short answer: No. It is fundamentally different, and that difference is the entire reason EMV exists.**

### 1.1 Magstripe (Track 1 / Track 2 — "101", "201" dumps in the carding lexicon)

- **What's captured:** the cardholder name, PAN, expiry, service code, and discretionary data — all printed verbatim on a magnetic stripe.
- **What it can do:** be **replayed indefinitely**. The data is static. Whoever holds it can re-encode it onto a blank card (or feed it into a magstripe-emulator device) and swipe it at any magstripe-accepting terminal until the issuer cancels the card. One skim = unlimited future fraud.
- **Why it works:** no per-transaction freshness. The terminal+issuer have no way of distinguishing "real first swipe" from "100th replay."

### 1.2 EMV contactless cryptograms (what `ckemv-harvester` captures)

- **What's captured:** PAN + AID + a **batch of 20-ish ARQC cryptograms**, each generated by the card for a different `(amount, date, currency, country, unpredictable-number)` tuple chosen by the attacker.
- **What an ARQC is:** a one-shot MAC, computed by the chip using a key only the chip and the issuer share, over the exact transaction parameters above plus an **ATC** (Application Transaction Counter) that the chip increments every single time it generates one. The ATC is the freshness anchor.
- **What an ARQC can do:** **be presented exactly once, to authorize one transaction whose parameters match (or are within tolerance of) the ones baked into the cryptogram.** Each subsequent transaction the issuer sees must have a higher ATC than the one before — and if the issuer ever sees a transaction with an ATC ≤ a prior one, that's an unambiguous fraud/replay signal.
- **Why batches of 20:** the harvester runs many cryptogram generations on the card while it's tapped, each with a different amount/date/etc., so the attacker has a *selection* of "shots" to choose from later. They can pick the cryptogram whose pre-baked parameters most closely match the merchant they're about to defraud.

### 1.3 Side-by-side

| Property | Magstripe Track 2 | EMV Contactless ARQC |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Static or dynamic | **Static** | **Dynamic, per-transaction** |
| Reuse count per capture | **Infinite** (until card cancelled) | **One** (each ARQC) |
| Capture yield per skim | **One snapshot, unlimited shots** | **~20 snapshots, ~20 shots** |
| Freshness check | None | Monotonic `ATC` counter |
| Parameter binding | None | Bound to amount/date/currency/UN/country |
| Window of validity | Until card cancelled (months) | Minutes to days for relay; until ATC overtaken |
| Cryptographic protection | None | 3DES or AES MAC, key never leaves chip |
| Issuer detection on replay | Hard | Trivial (ATC regression) |

### 1.4 So the magstripe analogy is wrong — what's the right one?

A captured ARQC is more like **a stolen Single-Use Voucher**. It has:
- A face value (the amount baked in)
- An expiry (driven by ATC drift and issuer replay-window policy)
- A merchant-category fingerprint (driven by the `9F35 Terminal Type`, `9F1A Country`, etc.)
- And it self-destructs the moment it's used.

If the criminal doesn't burn it in time, or a later legitimate tap by the cardholder advances the ATC past it, **the voucher is dead.**

This is exactly why the harvester *batches* — the attacker is buying themselves a small inventory of shots per card so at least one of them lands.

## 2. The two distinct monetization models

The single most important framing: **harvested cryptograms have only two ways to become money,** and they are mutually exclusive on the technical side.

### 2.1 Model A — Real-time relay (the "Ghost Tap" model — dominant today)

- The harvester phone in the victim's hand is **paired live with a confederate's phone** sitting next to a real contactless terminal at a real merchant.
- The criminal stands at the cash register. The confederate triggers the merchant terminal to begin a contactless transaction. The terminal sends APDUs to what it *thinks* is a contactless card; in reality, those APDUs are tunneled over the internet (Tor / VPN / commercial relay infrastructure) to the harvester phone, which **forwards them to the actual victim card** in real time, gets the responses back, and tunnels them back.
- The victim card is the one generating the cryptogram, *in the moment, for the actual merchant's transaction parameters.*
- Total latency budget: ~500 ms. EMV terminals tolerate up to a few seconds.
- **The 20 pre-harvested ARQCs in this model are essentially unused at relay time.** They're more of a backup/QA inventory the harvester gathered to confirm the card works and to give the operator side intelligence about the card's profile (network, AID, limits).

This is the model documented by ESET (NGate, 2024) and ThreatFabric (Ghost Tap, 2024). It's the one **the `ckemv` codebase is best suited for** — note how the kernel's state machine reads like a generic EMV-terminal driver, not a replay-attack engine.

### 2.2 Model B — Pre-play / store-and-forward (more niche, requires weak terminals)

- The harvester captures ARQCs ahead of time with parameters the attacker *guesses* a future merchant terminal will reproduce.
- The attacker uses a soft-PoS or compromised real terminal under their control to push the captured ARQC into the acquiring network.
- Works **only** against terminals that generate predictable Unpredictable Numbers (`9F37`) — i.e. low-entropy, sequential, time-based — and against issuers whose replay-window/ATC enforcement is lax.
- The Cambridge pre-play paper (2014) catalogued multiple real terminals in the wild whose UNs were predictable. Most have been patched, but pockets remain in legacy hardware, especially in non-tier-1 markets.
- The `deterministic_un=` switch in `libckemv_android.so`'s `initialize` log is **specifically the knob** that lets the operator demand a chosen `9F37` from the kernel — i.e. the operator can pre-play against a known-weak target.

Model B is rarer in 2025 because most acquirer-side issuance of terminals now uses hardware random-number generation. Model A subsumes most active campaigns.

## 3. Transaction-limit bypass: how do they get past "needs PIN"?

This is the most-asked question, and it has a layered answer. The short of it: **the contactless ceiling is enforced by the *terminal*, the PIN requirement is enforced by the *card's CVM list*, and the cumulative-amount limit is enforced by *both card and issuer*. Attackers attack the weakest of those three.**

### 3.1 What the limits actually are (2025 snapshot)

| Country / Region | Single-transaction contactless ceiling (no PIN) | Cumulative limit before PIN forced |
| --- | --- | --- |
| EU (most) | €50 | €150 over multiple sub-floor txns |
| UK | £100 | £300 cumulative |
| US | ~$100 (issuer / terminal varies, often higher) | varies wildly |
| India (RBI) | ₹5,000 | regulator-mandated PIN above |
| Australia | A$200 | A$200 single |
| Canada | C$250 | C$250 single |
| Japan | ¥10,000–¥30,000 (network-dependent) | varies |
| Brazil | R$200 | R$1,000 cumulative |

**Above the ceiling**, the terminal's risk-management logic flips to "CVM required" — meaning either a chip+PIN entry on the PINpad, or a CDCVM flag (see §3.4), or signature, or transaction declined.

### 3.2 Bypass family 1 — Sub-floor stacking

Don't fight the limit; live under it. The attacker chooses transaction amounts just below the floor (say £99.99 in the UK), and runs many such transactions across multiple merchants in rapid succession.

- **What stops it:** the card's `Lower Consecutive Offline Limit` and `Upper Consecutive Offline Limit` counters (`9F14`, `9F23`). Once exceeded, the card flips its CDOL1/CDOL2 response to *force-online* + *force-CVM*.
- **Issuer-side velocity rules** kick in fast: 5 contactless sub-floor charges in 10 minutes across geographically distant merchants is a textbook fraud-detection ML trigger.
- **Practical outcome:** the attacker typically gets 2–4 sub-floor swings per card before the issuer blocks. Combined with multiple harvested cards per operator-day, this is still highly profitable in aggregate.

### 3.3 Bypass family 2 — CVM downgrade attacks

The attacker (or a rogue softPoS terminal in their control) **rewrites the CVM result fields** in the transaction to claim "no CVM required" even on amounts above the floor.

- Galloway & Yunusov (Positive Technologies, Black Hat USA 2019 "First Contact: Vulnerabilities in Contactless Payments") demonstrated this against Visa contactless: a man-in-the-middle device strips the `TVR` (Terminal Verification Results) and `IAD` bits that say "PIN required" before the issuer sees them.
- Worked against Visa more reliably than Mastercard at the time, because Visa's offline-data-authentication mode (qVSDC) didn't sign the CVM-related TLVs the same way Mastercard's M/Chip did.
- Visa published mitigations; some issuers enforce server-side CVM cross-checks, others don't. **Field still has pockets that work.**
- **In `ckemv-harvester`'s context:** the harvester captures the raw card response. The downgrade happens on the *acquirer side* — i.e. when a confederate's softPoS submits the transaction with manipulated `TVR`. The harvester doesn't perform the downgrade itself; it provides the cryptogram that the softPoS frames as "no CVM needed."

### 3.4 Bypass family 3 — CDCVM (Consumer Device CVM) abuse

Mobile wallets (Apple Pay, Google Pay) and some smart cards have a flag called **CDCVM**: "the device already verified the cardholder, so the terminal doesn't need to."

- When a tokenized wallet transmits CDCVM-set in the cryptogram, the merchant terminal accepts the transaction *up to a much higher ceiling* (often £/€/$ thousands, or no ceiling).
- **Attack vector:** a softPoS/relay terminal under attacker control can set the CDCVM flag in the transaction even when the source was a plain plastic card that didn't actually verify anyone.
- Issuers are supposed to cross-check the `9F10 IAD` (Issuer Application Data) for actual CDCVM evidence, but enforcement is uneven — especially with cross-border acquirers.
- This is the *high-value* lane: a single CDCVM-flagged transaction can be in the thousands rather than capped at £100.

### 3.5 Bypass family 4 — Terminal type spoofing

Different `9F35 Terminal Type` and `9F33 Terminal Capabilities` values cause the card to skip cardholder verification entirely. ATMs and "unattended" terminals have different CVM rules than "attended POS." A rogue terminal that claims to be an unattended-fuel-pump terminal can pull approvals on cards that would refuse a clerk's POS.

### 3.6 What stops all of the above in practice

- **3D Secure / SCA for online/CNP:** for card-not-present (online) channels in PSD2-covered jurisdictions, two-factor (biometric/OTP) is mandated. Harvested cryptograms have **near-zero value** for online shopping in these jurisdictions because the OTP/biometric is what authorizes, not the cryptogram.
- **Issuer ML / behavioural scoring:** every major issuer now scores transactions in real time on geolocation, time-of-day, merchant-category drift, ATC drift, and dozens of other features. A relayed transaction usually fails one of these.
- **ATC sequence policing:** issuer-side enforcement of monotonic ATC is the cleanest defense. If the cardholder taps legitimately *after* a harvest, the legitimate ATC overtakes all of the harvester's stored cryptograms in one go and they're all immediately invalid.
- **Tokenization** (see §5).

## 4. Regional / geographic blocks and how attackers route around them

### 4.1 What the blocks actually are

1. **Issuer geo-rules:** "this card cannot be used outside India" — set by cardholder via the bank app, or by the bank by default. Evaluated **by the issuer host** when it sees the authorization. The terminal/acquirer doesn't enforce it.
2. **BIN-range blocks at acquirer:** some acquirers refuse transactions from certain BINs (issuer-country) to limit cross-border exposure.
3. **Scheme-level routing:** Visa Direct, Mastercard Send, etc. have country-pair allow/deny lists for push-payments.
4. **Sanctioned-jurisdiction blocks** (OFAC etc.) — enforced at acquirer and scheme level.

### 4.2 How the criminal ecosystem routes around them

- **Geographic mule networks.** A harvester operator in country X uploads cryptograms to the C2; a *confederate* in the cardholder's home country runs the relay against a merchant there. The card never appears "out of country" from the issuer's perspective because the merchant is local. This is the dominant pattern in ESET/ThreatFabric writeups.
- **Cross-border acquirer arbitrage.** Some acquiring banks (often in tax-havens or less-regulated jurisdictions) front transactions for merchants in arbitrary countries. The transaction shows up to the issuer as coming from acquirer-country Y even though the merchant is in country Z.
- **Synthetic merchant location.** A softPoS in attacker control can submit transactions with attacker-chosen `9F1A Terminal Country Code` and merchant-category-code. Acquirer compliance varies — many don't validate that the submitted country matches the acquirer's known merchant address.
- **Fallback to lax issuers.** Cards issued by smaller banks with limited fraud-ops capacity are preferred targets. Operator dashboards segregate cards by BIN ↔ issuer ↔ historical-success.

### 4.3 What the harvested cryptogram *itself* tells the attacker for routing

Remember: the harvester's 20-shot batch was generated **with attacker-chosen `9F1A Terminal Country Code`, `9F35 Terminal Type`, currency `5F2A`**, etc. So the operator already knows, before they relay:
- This cryptogram was generated assuming a `<country code>` terminal — try to relay at a merchant in that country to minimize cross-border friction.
- This cryptogram was for `<amount>` — pick a merchant where that amount looks unsurprising.

The harvester is essentially **pre-staging the criminal transaction** at capture time.

## 5. Tokenization — Apple Pay, Google Pay, Samsung Pay, and country wallets

This is where the answer changes substantially.

### 5.1 What tokenization actually is

When you add a card to Apple Pay / Google Pay / Samsung Pay / Garmin Pay / Fitbit Pay, the wallet provider asks the issuer (via the scheme's Token Service Provider — Visa VTS, Mastercard MDES, AmEx Token Service) to issue a **DPAN** (Device Primary Account Number) — a 16-digit number that looks like a card number but is **not** your real PAN.

- The DPAN is **bound** to that specific device's hardware secure element (Apple Secure Enclave, Android Strongbox/TEE, Samsung Knox).
- Cryptograms are generated by the *secure element on the phone*, not by your real card chip.
- The secure element's signing key is provisioned during enrollment and never leaves.
- Every contactless tap from the phone returns a DPAN + a fresh cryptogram from the secure element.

### 5.2 What `ckemv-harvester` can and can't get from a tokenized wallet

**What it can get if the victim taps a tokenized phone against the harvester:**

- The DPAN (looks like a card number but is functionally useless outside the original device's binding)
- A single ARQC, signed by the wallet's secure element with the DPAN's per-token key
- The `9F26 ARQC` is real and cryptographically valid

**Why this is much less useful to the criminal:**

- The DPAN is bound to the *device* and the *Token Requestor ID* (TRID — Apple Pay has its own TRID, Google Pay its own, etc.). When relayed to a softPoS, the transaction arrives at the TSP carrying a TRID. If the TRID doesn't match the merchant/acquirer profile expected for that DPAN (e.g., DPAN belongs to Apple Pay TRID, but the relayed transaction claims an in-store Mastercard contactless TRID), the TSP rejects.
- **Dynamic Token Cryptogram (DTC):** the cryptogram is also bound to a TSP-issued nonce, not just the card-side UN. Replay against a different terminal context fails the TSP's validation, even if the issuer's own ATC check passes.
- **Token can be revoked in seconds** by the wallet provider (e.g., "Find My iPhone → Remove from Wallet") — much faster than issuing a new physical card.
- **CDCVM is real on the wallet side.** Apple Pay requires Face ID/Touch ID/passcode to generate any cryptogram. A harvester can't get the wallet to generate a cryptogram without the device user actually authenticating *on the device itself* at tap time.

### 5.3 The remaining attack surface on wallets

- **Pre-authenticated wallet timing window:** Apple Pay holds an "express transit" mode for transit cards (no biometric needed). A harvester reading a victim's iPhone in transit-pay mode would get a transit-purposed cryptogram — but those are MCC-locked to transit operators and almost useless outside that category.
- **Social engineering to authenticate.** This is what **SuperCard X** does (Cleafy, 2024-25): a separate Android malware tricks the victim into installing a fake "bank verification" app, which uses Android's HCE (Host Card Emulation) to emulate the victim's tokenized card and relay it live. The cryptograms are issued *by the victim's wallet* because the victim authenticated when prompted (thinking they were verifying with the bank).
- **Provisioning fraud.** Stealing OTPs to enroll the victim's card into the *attacker's* wallet (so the attacker becomes the device-bound holder). Then *no* live victim card is needed at all.

### 5.4 The honest bottom line on tokenization

**Tokenization is the single most effective defense against `ckemv-harvester`-class attacks.** A tokenized wallet:
- Limits cryptogram value to single-use, TRID-bound
- Adds device-binding the harvester can't replicate
- Enables instant revocation
- Forces CDCVM on every tap

This is why the **active criminal ecosystem is moving away from harvesting physical cards and toward (a) social-engineering wallet enrollment fraud and (b) realtime ghost-tap relay against non-tokenized plastic cards in markets with low wallet penetration.**

`ckemv-harvester`'s target population is **physical-plastic contactless cards in markets with significant non-wallet contactless usage** — i.e. EU, UK, parts of LATAM, India (for plastic cards), much of MENA. Wallet-heavy markets (US iPhone users, urban EU Apple Pay users) are *less* exposed to this specific tool.

## 6. Practical applicability — does this actually work in 2025?

Yes, with caveats. Public threat-intel from ESET/ThreatFabric/Cleafy throughout 2024 documents **active, profitable** campaigns using exactly this architecture (harvester app + relay terminal + C2 SaaS).

### 6.1 What a realistic operator day looks like (reconstructed from public reporting)

1. Operator picks up a burner Android phone pre-flashed with `Geometry Saver`.
2. Operator pastes their `api_key` (collector identity).
3. Operator engages with marks via:
   - Voice-phishing scam: *"This is your bank's fraud department, please tap your card to your phone to confirm the most recent transaction."*
   - Fake-courier scam: *"You have a parcel pickup, tap your card here to verify identity."*
   - Crowd skimming in transit hubs.
4. Each successful tap = ~20 cryptograms uploaded to `tqqemv.xyz` within 10 seconds.
5. Cryptograms are consumed within minutes-to-hours by a confederate at a real merchant abroad (relay model) or by a softPoS in attacker control.
6. Operator gets a percentage cut, paid in crypto, weekly.

### 6.2 Yield per harvest tap (industry estimates from public reporting)

- **Probability that any of the 20 cryptograms successfully monetizes:** ~30–70%, depending on card brand, issuer, and CVM-bypass success.
- **Average loss per successfully exploited card:** £200–£2,000 (driven by CVM-downgrade and sub-floor stacking).
- **Per-operator daily yield (anecdotal, from Cleafy/ThreatFabric writeups):** several thousand £/€ on a productive day, with high variance.

### 6.3 What makes it work

- **Wide deployment of HCE on Android.** Any Android phone can become a soft-POS or a relay endpoint. No special hardware needed.
- **Affordable softPoS hardware in legit market (Stripe Tap to Pay, Square Tap, Adyen Tap to Pay) creates a credible cover for rogue softPoS at the relay end.**
- **Acquirer compliance heterogeneity.** Tier-1 acquirers (Stripe, Adyen, Worldpay) are tight; smaller acquirers globally are not.
- **Issuer fraud-ops capacity asymmetry.** Megabanks have ML scoring; smaller institutions don't.
- **Speed of execution.** The whole capture-to-burn cycle is minutes, faster than the cardholder can notice and call the bank.

### 6.4 What constrains it

- **Tokenization where adopted.** See §5.
- **Issuer ATC monotonicity enforcement.** Where strict, harvested ARQCs go stale within hours.
- **3D Secure / SCA mandates** killing the online-channel monetization route in PSD2 jurisdictions.
- **Real-time geo-velocity rules** at issuers.
- **Cardholder behaviour.** Cardholders who don't tap unknown devices, who carry RFID-shielded wallets, or who use only tokenized wallets are immune.
- **Mobile-AV / EDR / MDM detection** of the harvester APK itself (see the IOC index in the companion report).

## 8. ATC and the real reuse window

The single most important clock here is the ATC. Worked example, simplified:

1. Victim's card has ATC = 0x0041 (65 in decimal) when harvested.
2. Harvester records 20 cryptograms — they cover ATC 0x0042 through 0x0055.
3. Each of those 20 is independently usable, *but the issuer expects them in strictly ascending order.*
4. Victim taps card legitimately the next morning at a coffee shop. Card increments to 0x0056. Issuer logs ATC 0x0056 cleared.
5. **Every one of the harvester's 20 stored cryptograms is now stale**, because their ATCs (0x0042–0x0055) are all below the issuer's last-seen ATC. A correctly-implemented issuer host rejects them outright.

This is why the harvest-to-burn window is **measured in hours, not days, in practice** — and why the criminal pipeline is built for speed (10-second upload loop, near-realtime relay, no manual processing).

It's also why issuer-side ATC monotonicity is the **single highest-leverage defense** the issuer can deploy. Cards that monotonically enforce ATC and issuer hosts that monotonically enforce ATC kill ~70% of the harvester's inventory value.

## 9. Defender's bottom line

**For issuers:**
1. Enforce strict ATC monotonicity, with no tolerance window beyond a small forward gap.
2. Real-time velocity scoring on contactless transactions; auto-decline anything outside the cardholder's geo-profile.
3. CVM cross-check in IAD — never trust terminal-reported "no CVM" without IAD evidence.
4. Push tokenization aggressively. Subsidize Apple Pay/Google Pay onboarding.
5. Issue alerts to cardholder on every contactless transaction above a threshold (free push, free SMS).

**For acquirers:**
1. Mandatory `9F1A` ↔ acquirer-of-record country consistency.
2. Refuse cross-border submissions that don't carry valid TRID/TSP routing.
3. Mandatory submission of TVR/CVM-result fields end-to-end.

**For cardholders:**
1. Tokenize. Stop using plastic contactless if your card-issuer supports Apple Pay / Google Pay.
2. Don't tap your card to a phone or device handed to you by a stranger or "bank rep on the phone."
3. RFID-shielded wallet for cards you can't tokenize.
4. Enable per-transaction notifications.
5. If you suspect a tap, call the issuer immediately — every minute of delay is fresh ARQC inventory the criminal can burn.

**For corporate security:**
1. MDM-block sideloaded packages matching the IOCs in `CKEMV_HARVESTER_ANALYSIS.md` §10.
2. Network-level sinkhole `tqqemv.xyz` and related infrastructure.
3. Train against the social-engineering scripts (fake bank verification calls).

## 10. The honest answer to the user's headline question

> *"They are storing it and can reuse them whenever they want, like a magstripe 101?"*

**No.** Each captured cryptogram is a one-shot, parameter-bound, freshness-checked voucher with an expiry measured in hours and a usability that collapses the moment the cardholder taps legitimately again. The harvester captures **a batch (~20)** to give the operator a small inventory of shots per card, and the **entire criminal pipeline is built for speed** (10-second upload, near-realtime relay) because the inventory decays so fast.

The bypasses of PIN/CVM limits exist but are not magic — they exploit specific terminal/issuer implementation laxity (CVM downgrade, CDCVM abuse, sub-floor stacking, terminal-type spoofing), and the industry has been progressively closing each one. Regional blocks are routed around with **mule networks of confederates**, not with technical bypasses.

Tokenized wallets (Apple Pay, Google Pay) **substantially defeat** this attack class — the criminal ecosystem has accordingly shifted toward attacking wallet *enrollment* (provisioning fraud) and wallet *authentication* (CDCVM social engineering) rather than chasing tokenized cryptograms.

Practically, the tool is *most valuable* against plastic contactless cards in jurisdictions where tokenization is light, SCA isn't mandatory, and the operator has a domestic relay confederate. That's a smaller-than-it-was footprint, but still wide enough to support active, profitable campaigns in 2025.
